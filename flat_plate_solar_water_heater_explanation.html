Stage 1: Energy Input

The collector area is 0.34 m² — compact enough for residential installation, large enough to capture meaningful power.
At 1000 W/m² irradiance, the panel intercepts 341 watts of solar power. That's roughly equivalent to three incandescent bulbs, but continuous and free.
Context
1000 W/m² is standard test condition irradiance — what you'd measure on a clear day with the sun directly overhead. Our system is sized for typical residential rooftop constraints.

Stage 3: The Absorber

⬛
The transmitted radiation hits a black-painted aluminum plate. Material selection here is deliberate: black for maximum absorptivity, aluminum for thermal conductivity.
The black coating absorbs 80% of incident radiation. After accounting for glazing losses, the plate receives 232W of thermal input — this is where optical energy becomes heat.

Material Properties
Black surfaces have high absorptivity across the visible spectrum. The aluminum substrate (k = 237 W/m·K) ensures uniform temperature distribution — preventing hot spots that would increase radiative losses.
The 5mm aluminum plate spreads heat laterally before transferring it to the water channels. Thermal conductivity matters here.

Stage 4: Heat Exchange

Bonded to the plate's underside: 8 parallel copper tubes in a serpentine configuration. Copper (k = 399 W/m·K) ensures minimal thermal resistance between plate and fluid.
System Specifications:
• 8 parallel channels for flow distribution
• 4.22 meters total tube length
• Type L copper tubing (3/8" diameter)
• Convection coefficient: 108 W/(m²·K)
Water enters at 25°C. As it flows through the heated tubes, convective heat transfer raises the bulk fluid temperature. Flow rate and residence time determine the temperature gain.
Tin = 25°C → Tout = 38.5°C
Temperature Rise
The 13.5°C increase represents the net heat transfer from plate to water. Higher flow rates would decrease ΔT but increase total energy transfer — there's an optimization curve here.

Calculation (with heat losses)
The system delivers:
• Qin = 190.9 W (heat input to water)
• Qout = 22.4 W (heat loss to ambient)
• Net heat = 168.5 W

dT/dt = (Qin - Qout) / (m × cp)
= 168.5 W / (7.06 kg × 4182 J/kg·K)
= 0.0057 K/s (theoretical)
= 0.0048 K/s (from numerical model)
Note: Heating rate decreases over time as the reservoir approaches steady-state (38.5°C), since heat losses increase with temperature.
♻️
Continuous Operation
Water circulates through the system in a closed loop. As long as solar irradiance remains constant, the reservoir maintains steady-state temperature with ongoing thermal input.
